About Body Mass Index (BMI)

BMI serves as a valuable tool in assessing overweight and obesity, derived from the measurements of your height and weight. This calculation provides an estimation of body fat and serves as a reliable indicator of your susceptibility to diseases associated with increased body fat. A higher BMI correlates with an elevated risk of various conditions, including heart disease, high blood pressure, type 2 diabetes, gallstones, breathing difficulties, and specific types of cancers.

While BMI is generally applicable to most individuals, it does possess certain limitations:

  • Athletes and individuals with a well-developed muscular physique may observe an overestimation of body fat when using BMI as it does not account for the distinction between muscle and fat mass.
  • Older individuals and those who have experienced muscle loss may witness an underestimation of body fat due to reduced muscle mass, which can affect the accuracy of the BMI calculation.

These limitations highlight the need for a comprehensive approach when evaluating an individual's health and weight status, taking into account factors beyond BMI alone.

*Reference: National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute

How is BMI Calculated?

Kilograms and meters (or centimeters)

Formula: weight (kg) / [height (m)]2

Example: Weight = 68 kg, Height = 165 cm (1.65 m)

Calculation: 68 ÷ (1.65)2 = 24.98

Pounds and inches

Formula: weight (lb) / [height (in)]2 x 703

Example: Weight = 150 lbs, Height = 5’5″ (65″)

Calculation: [150 ÷ (65)2] x 703 = 24.96

Risk Factors Associated with Obesity

  • High blood pressure (hypertension)
  • High LDL cholesterol ("bad" cholesterol)
  • Low HDL cholesterol ("good" cholesterol)
  • High triglycerides
  • High blood glucose (sugar)
  • Family history of premature heart disease
  • Physical inactivity
  • Cigarette smoking

*Reference: National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute
